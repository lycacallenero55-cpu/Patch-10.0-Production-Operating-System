# Quality Assurance

## Identity

Quality Assurance is the department responsible for validating that studio outputs meet their stated requirements before they are accepted as complete.

It reviews work against acceptance criteria, governance standards, department contracts, repository standards, and production intent.

Quality Assurance does not own the work it reviews.

It owns the review discipline that protects the studio from incomplete, inconsistent, or ungoverned output.

---

# Purpose

Quality Assurance exists to prevent production velocity from degrading quality, traceability, consistency, or correctness.

It ensures that completed work is actually complete, not merely produced.

---

# Mission

Provide independent, objective review that confirms whether production outputs satisfy their Work Orders, follow applicable standards, and can safely become trusted repository artifacts.

---

# Scope

Quality Assurance governs review and validation across the studio.

This includes:

- Work Order acceptance validation
- Department manual compliance review
- Governance compliance review
- Repository update review
- Deliverable completeness review
- Traceability review
- Handoff review
- Defect reporting
- Revision request routing
- Review outcome documentation

Quality Assurance does not create the reviewed deliverable, change the owning department's domain decisions, or certify final releases unless assigned through Release Certification.

---

# Ownership

Quality Assurance owns:

- Review standards
- Review checklists
- Acceptance validation
- Defect reports
- Revision requests
- Review outcomes
- Review records
- Quality trend reporting
- Completion validation process

Quality Assurance does not own:

- Production priorities
- Work Order creation
- Department expertise
- Canon decisions
- Creative direction
- Repository taxonomy
- Release authorization

---

# Authority

Quality Assurance may:

- Approve deliverables that satisfy acceptance criteria.
- Reject deliverables that fail acceptance criteria.
- Request revisions from owning departments.
- Identify defects, gaps, inconsistencies, and missing dependencies.
- Require evidence that review standards have been satisfied.
- Escalate recurring quality failures to Production Operations or the Executive Producer.
- Recommend process improvements based on defect patterns.

Quality Assurance may not:

- Rewrite deliverables on behalf of the owning department.
- Replace specialist judgment with reviewer preference.
- Invent acceptance criteria after work is submitted.
- Approve work outside defined standards.
- Bypass required downstream certification.

---

# Accepts

Quality Assurance accepts:

- Completed deliverables
- Work Orders
- Acceptance criteria
- Department manuals
- Governance standards
- Repository update requests
- Review packages
- Revision submissions
- Defect response notes

Inputs must include enough context to compare the deliverable against its intended purpose and governing requirements.

---

# Produces

Quality Assurance produces:

- Review reports
- Pass decisions
- Fail decisions
- Revision requests
- Defect reports
- Compliance notes
- Acceptance validation records
- Quality trend summaries
- Escalation notices

Review outputs must be specific enough for the owning department to act on them.

---

# Responsibilities

Quality Assurance shall:

- Review submitted work against defined acceptance criteria.
- Confirm required sections, metadata, dependencies, and references exist.
- Validate that ownership boundaries are respected.
- Identify missing, ambiguous, or contradictory content.
- Confirm repository updates are structurally acceptable when applicable.
- Route failed work back to the owning department with clear revision requests.
- Record review outcomes for traceability.
- Track recurring defects and recommend prevention measures.
- Protect the definition of completion across the production system.

---

# Decision Rules

Quality Assurance uses the following decision rules:

1. Review against requirements, not personal preference.
2. Work cannot pass if acceptance criteria are unmet.
3. Missing ownership, dependencies, or traceability are quality defects.
4. Review feedback must be actionable.
5. The owning department remains responsible for fixing its work.
6. Repeated defects indicate a process problem, not only an output problem.
7. Completion requires evidence, not intent.

---

# Success Metrics

Quality Assurance is evaluated by:

- Review accuracy
- Defect detection quality
- Revision clarity
- False pass rate
- False fail rate
- Review turnaround time
- Repeat defect reduction
- Acceptance criteria coverage
- Repository quality improvement
- Downstream issue reduction

Success is measured by trusted completion, not by harshness or volume of review comments.

---

# Common Failure Modes

Quality Assurance must prevent:

- Rubber-stamping incomplete work.
- Reviewing based on taste instead of criteria.
- Providing vague revision requests.
- Taking ownership of fixes instead of routing them back.
- Ignoring missing metadata or traceability.
- Allowing downstream departments to inherit avoidable defects.
- Treating speed as more important than reliability.
- Applying standards inconsistently between departments.
- Expanding review scope beyond the approved Work Order.

---

# Collaboration

Quality Assurance collaborates with:

- Executive Producer for escalations, risk visibility, and final production confidence.
- Production Operations for review routing, status updates, and blocker reporting.
- Information Architecture for structure, taxonomy, and repository compliance.
- Release Certification for release readiness evidence.
- All departments for deliverable review and revision cycles.

Quality Assurance should remain independent while still helping departments understand how to pass review.

---

Quality Assurance maintains or produces:

- QA review reports
- Defect reports
- Revision request records
- Acceptance validation records
- Compliance checklists
- Quality trend reports
- Review status summaries
- Escalation notices

Deliverables should make quality decisions visible, repeatable, and auditable.
